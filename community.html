Host communities come first.

The corridor model centers local reuse, brownfield reinvestment, workforce development planning, and clear public communication — with commitments tracked, engagement recorded, and public claims held to cited sources.

Community benefits

Benefits are planned, tracked, and source-backed.

Community benefit planning is tied to approved project scopes and authorized public statements — not aspirations. Each commitment gets a tracker entry with an owner and status.

Tracked

Benefit trackers

Source-backed benefit planning tied to approved scopes, so each commitment can be reviewed and verified.

Approved commitments

Workforce & local impact

Engagement

Engagement that leaves a record.

What engagement includes

  • Engagement plans and outreach schedules
  • Approved talking points and plain-language materials
  • Stakeholder coordination with access controls where needed
  • Source-backed summaries of meetings and requests
  • Feedback loops and host-community commitments

Environmental justice context

Each site file carries community and EJ context — documented alongside regulatory, infrastructure, and feedstock records so community considerations stay visible in every planning decision.

ⓘ Placeholder discipline: if outreach language or relationship claims depend on missing source documents, the gap is noted clearly instead of inventing content.
Public communication

What residents can expect from us.

Plain language

Public materials are written to be read — not to obscure.

Documented meetings

Meetings produce governed notes: attendees, decisions, open questions, follow-ups.

No overstated claims

Jobs, benefits, and outcomes are stated only with source support.

Visible commitments

Approved commitments are tracked to completion with named owners.

Feedback routes

Community questions have a documented path to an answer.

Local reuse focus

Planning favors local material reuse and brownfield reinvestment.
